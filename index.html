Case Study 01 · Western Union · 2023–2025

Reducing Card Payment
Abandonment by 18%

How going deep on data and agent behaviour — before touching a single solution — uncovered the real problem and drove measurable results across 14+ European markets.

CompanyWestern Union
Markets14+ European
My RoleProduct Manager
Outcome18% Abandonment Reduction
01 · Context

The Problem

At Western Union retail locations across Europe, customers come in to send money cross-border using debit and credit cards at the point of sale. These are high-value, in-person transactions where both reliability and fraud prevention are critical.

We started seeing higher-than-expected transaction failures and abandonment during the payment step. The instinct would be to jump straight to fixes — but that's exactly what I didn't do.

My Approach
"Before touching a solution, I needed to understand what was actually happening — not what we assumed was happening."
02 · Discovery

Understanding Behaviour First

I instrumented the payment flow and used Quantum Metric to replay real transactions — observing how agents actually handled declines in live scenarios across our highest-volume markets.

This surfaced an insight that wasn't visible in the raw decline data alone:

Key Insight
The issue wasn't just the declines themselves — it was the absence of actionable guidance. Agents were seeing generic error messages and couldn't tell whether to retry, switch cards, or stop due to fraud risk. This caused both customer drop-offs and unnecessary risk exposure.

This distinction mattered enormously. A solution targeting "decline rates" would have missed the real problem entirely. The real problem was a decision support gap at the point of failure.

03 · Prioritisation

Data-Driven Prioritisation

There were many different decline reasons contributing to failures. Rather than trying to solve everything at once, I analysed trends across our largest markets and identified the top five highest-impact decline categories to address first.

This was a deliberate choice. Trying to fix every decline type simultaneously would have delayed impact and spread engineering effort too thin. Prioritising the top five meant we could ship fast, measure, and then expand coverage based on real results.

1
Analyse decline data across all major markets
Mapped decline reasons by volume, frequency, and commercial impact across 14+ European markets.
2
Identify top 5 highest-impact categories
Selected categories where actionable guidance would have the greatest effect on abandonment and risk exposure.
3
Partner with Risk to classify into tiers
Worked with the Risk team to classify each category: block / safe retry / switch to alternative method.
4
Embed guidance directly into POS workflow
Partnered with Engineering to surface clear, context-specific guidance at the exact moment of decline.
5
Incremental rollout → measure → expand
Released market by market, measured agent behaviour and transaction outcomes, then expanded coverage iteratively.
04 · Outcome

What We Achieved

The iterative, data-first approach delivered measurable results across all markets — not just in the short term, but sustainably, because each rollout was validated before expansion.

18%
Reduction in card payment abandonment across high-value retail transactions
14+
European markets covered with structured decline classification
E2E
Full ownership from discovery and prioritisation through delivery and post-launch optimisation
05 · Reflection

What I Learned

The most important decision I made on this project was the first one: to not jump to solutions. Most PM instincts — and most stakeholder pressure — push towards speed. But speed without understanding leads to solving the wrong problem.

The second thing I learned: the data you don't have is as important as the data you do. Session replays and agent observation surfaced the guidance gap — something no decline report would have shown. Understanding the limits of your data is a core PM skill.

And finally: iterating based on evidence, rather than shipping everything at once, was what made the improvement durable. Each market rollout taught us something that made the next one better.

Case Study 02 · Cyber Resilience Platform · 2025

Improving Human-AI
Interaction

How I identified four critical UX gaps in an ML-powered ransomware detection platform — and proposed a redesign grounded in Explainable AI principles.

IndustryCyber Security
Product TypeEnterprise AI Platform
My RoleSenior PM — UX
FocusExplainable AI (XAI)
01 · Context

The Product

I work as Senior PM on an enterprise cyber resilience platform that uses machine learning to detect ransomware attacks. The system compares file system snapshots across backup cycles, identifying attack patterns before they cause irreversible data loss.

I own the complete UX and workflow strategy. This case study focuses on the alert and investigation experience — where ML model output meets human decision-making under pressure.

The Core Challenge
The ML detection engine is highly accurate — but its outputs are not self-explanatory to the security admin who must act on them, often in the middle of the night during a live attack.
02 · How It Works

The Detection Pipeline

Understanding the system at a product level is essential context. Here's how the ML detection works:

1
Snapshot Comparison
Compares today's backup against the previous one, tracking every file-level change across the system.
2
Signal Extraction
Generates a large set of statistical signals from the comparison — file behaviour patterns, size changes, and type distributions.
3
Anomaly Detection
ML model analyses signals to identify patterns characteristic of ransomware, such as mass file encryption or unusual deletions.
4
ML Model Prediction
A trained ML model combines all signals to predict whether an attack has occurred, with a confidence score indicating likelihood.
5
Alert Generation
Outputs alerts with severity levels and file-level detail into the application UI for security admin review.
03 · Problems Identified

Four Gaps I Found

Through close analysis of the live alert UI and deep understanding of how the ML system works, I independently identified four interconnected problems widening the gap between model output and human decision.

01
Alert Fatigue
Thousands of alerts across hundreds of pages. When the system flags everything at the same severity, the labels become meaningless and admins face decision paralysis.
02
Explainability Gap
Raw ML scores displayed to security admins under pressure don't answer the only question that matters: should I recover this file right now?
03
Workflow Fragmentation
The user's job is to recover from an attack — but threat details and last clean backup are never shown together, forcing stressful navigation at the worst moment.
04
No Progressive Disclosure
Critical and High severity look nearly identical as text labels. No visual hierarchy pulls attention to what genuinely requires immediate action.
04 · User Journey

Before vs. After

The user's job-to-be-done isn't "read alerts" — it's "recover from a ransomware attack as fast as possible." Here's how the current journey fails that goal:

Current Journey — Broken
Open alerts tab → thousands of undifferentiated rows
Scroll through hundreds of pages to find real threat
Click alert → see raw ML score with no guidance
Navigate separately to find last clean backup
Make recovery decision with incomplete context
Proposed Journey — Fixed
Dashboard surfaces critical incidents with visual severity
Colour-coded indicators replace raw ML scores
Last clean backup shown inline with each alert
Action labels: "Recover Now" / "Investigate" / "Monitor"
Recovery decision made in one screen, under 2 minutes
05 · Proposed Solutions

What I'd Build

Solution 1 — Colour-Coded ML Confidence Indicators

Replace raw model scores with a three-tier visual system mapped to recommended actions. The core principle of Explainable AI: don't show the model's math — show what the user should do.

Visual IndicatorML Confidence LevelRecommended Action
Critical
 High confidence — attack highly likely Recover immediately — show last clean backup inline
Suspicious
 Moderate confidence — anomaly detected Investigate further before recovering
Clean
 Low confidence — likely safe No action needed — safe to use for recovery

Solution 2 — Unified Recovery Dashboard

Redesign the homepage to surface critical alerts grouped by attack event, the last confirmed clean backup per host, and a one-click recovery initiation — collapsing a multi-screen workflow into a single decision surface.

Solution 3 — Incident Grouping

A single attack affecting thousands of files should surface as one high-priority incident — not thousands of rows. Grouping by attack event with a visual severity summary directly addresses alert fatigue and mirrors how leading security platforms present ML threat intelligence.

06 · Validation Plan

How I'd Test This

1
Usability Testing with Security Admins
Observe 3–5 enterprise admins using the current alert view during a simulated attack scenario. Measure time-to-decision and error rate as baseline.
2
Define Success Metrics
Mean time to recovery decision · Alert dismissal rate · False positive acknowledgement rate · Admin confidence score (self-reported)
3
A/B Test Alert Grouping
Individual row view vs. incident-grouped view. Measure impact on response time and rate of missed critical alerts.
